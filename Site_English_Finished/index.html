What is virtual reality

Virtual reality is a virtually generated representation of the real world or an imaginary world. These alternate realities allow the user to interact with them and most of these realities create impressive sensory experiences, this is with the intention of immersing the user into the virtual reality. Some advanced haptic systems now include tactile information, generally knows as Force feedback in medical, gaming and military applications. On top of this virtual realities cover remote communication environments that provide the virtual presence of users with concepts of telepresence and tele-existence or a Virtual Artifact (VA) either by the well-known keyboard and mouse or by using the recent wired glove or omnidirectional treadmills.

A short history

First mentions of virtual reality

Virtual reality originated from the world of science fiction. In the short story “Pygmalion’s Spectacles” by author Stanley G. Weinbaum in 1935, the first traces of virtual reality can be found. The first mention of the term virtual reality can be found in the French collection of essays, translated as ‘The Theatre and its Double’ by Antonin Artaud in 1938. Since then there were few mentions of virtual reality.

Gaining popularity

Mass media managed to popularize the concept of virtual reality with movies such as Brainstorm (1983), The Lawnmower Man (1992) and The Matrix (1999). Virtual reality in its modern usage was promoted through the company VPL Research. The company held many VR patents in the 80’s. They developed the first widely used hardware for virtual reality: The HMD: EyePhone and the Haptic Input Data Glove.

VR movement tracking prototypes

The DataGlove was equipped with optical flex sensors. These sensors had fibre optic cables with a light at one end, and a photodiode at the other. Its purpose was tracking hand movements. This device originally started as an input system for computers and later used for virtual reality systems. Applications for this wearable device centred on input computer control, gaming, and also the potential for remote surgery. This was the first movement tracking glove available on the market, however it came with a price tag of $9000. The EyePhone was a head mounted display (HMD) that was meant to immerse users into a computer simulation. It could track head movements.

Devices simulating virtual reality

Morton Heilig wrote in the 1950s about an ‘Experience Theatre’. He thought of a way to turn watching movies into a more immersive experience. He wanted to make the user feel as if he were actually in the film. He made his dream come true and build a machine that combined projected film, audio, vibration, wind and odours. In 1962 the theatre machine was born, dubbed the Sensorama. The viewer could experience one of five immersive two-minute 3D full colour films. In 1968 the first virtual reality and augmented reality (AR) head-mounted display (HMD) system was created. The HMD to be worn by the user was so heavy that it had to be suspended from the ceiling. The formidable appearance of the device inspired its name, The Sword of Damocles.

90’s VR devices

A number of video game development companies brought their virtual reality products on the market in the 90’s. Japanese multinational video game company SEGA launched the Sega VR headset for arcade games and the Mega Drive console in 1991. The company Virtuality came up with their multiplayer VR entertainment system. They featured headsets and exoskeleton gloves. By 1994, Sega released the Sega VR-1 motion simulator arcade attraction. Nintendo released the Virtual Boy in Japan IN 1995. Forte released the VFX1, a PC-powered virtual reality headset in 1995.

Current and upcoming VR devices

In 2010, Palmer Luckey, who later went on to found Oculus VR, designed the first prototype of the Oculus Rift. This prototype, built on a shell of another virtual reality headset, displayed only 2-D images and was noticeably cumbersome to wear. However, it boasted a 90-degree field of vision that was previously unseen anywhere in the market at the time. This initial design would later serve as a basis from which the later designs came. In July 2013, Guild Software's Vendetta Online was widely reported as the first MMORPG to support the Oculus Rift, making it potentially the first persistent online world with native support for a consumer virtual reality headset. Google announces Google Cardboard, stereoscopic viewer for smartphone users.

What VR is now

How is virtual reality currently used?

Virtual reality is currently used in a very primitive form to provide simulations for medical, military, educational and recreational purposes. Some of the largest reasons virtual reality is still in an undeveloped state are that creating a virtual reality device is very time consuming and expensive. On top of that, the systems created at this moment do not possess a user-friendly interface. Recently there have been more people trying to develop the virtual reality, because of this it will probably start evolving soon.

The future of VR

The future of virtual reality, what will it be like?

Virtual reality is a revolutionary concept in multiple fields, which include medical, military, entertainment and education. These fields would each utilize it differently but efficiently, the virtual revolution will probably begin if the possibility to have your mind enter the virtual reality without using your body is developed. For the medical sector this would allow for patients to get into the virtual reality during treatment or surgeries to not require anaesthetics. Doctors would also be able to use the virtual reality to simulate diseases and develop treatments without having to use actual resources like pills or medicines to test new treatments as this can be done inside the virtual reality. The military world would use the system for more efficient and realistic combat simulations. They would be able to reproduce multiple scenarios where the soldiers have survive in the best way possible. Lastly the gaming world would completely turn around. The players would send their mind into the game to control their character like they would with their own body to play the game. Each one of these sectors would use the new technology to the best of their ability. However the problem with being able to send your mind into a virtual space without using your body also would mean your body is defenceless during that time, this gives rise to moral and ethical questions on the topic.
